There are also vegetarian options.\n\nThinius says the meals appeal to working parents with kids to feed and to busy business travelers who perhaps don't want to go cold turkey after several days of tucking into delicious airplane food.\n\nMORE: Is inflight fine dining possible?\n\nAir Food One's meals are better than the inflight equivalent, he insists, because they're delivered fresh for customers to heat up themselves instead of being precooked and packed into the metal crates used by commercial aviation caterers.\n\nThere's also the advantage of being able to eat them with real metal cutlery and -- depending on the customer's domestic setup -- without being repeatedly jostled by the person in the next seat.\n\n\"They're astonishingly OK,\" says Thinius. \"I didn't think one could do such stuff that you heat and eat it, but it works.\n\n\"It's not as good as if you cook yourself -- unless you can't cook properly -- but it's OK.\"\n\nThe pilot service appears to be going well so far, he adds, but it's still too early to tell if it'll be rolled out further afield.\n\n\"If it will be successful we will of course expand, if it's not, we won't. There's a lot of things we have to test, not just if the meals are liked by the customers, but also the logistics.\"\n\nSo for the time being, the rest of us will have to suffer the inconvenience of long-haul travel if we're hungry for inflight flavors.\n\nOr we could just settle for a sandwich.\n\nWould you eat airline food at home?
