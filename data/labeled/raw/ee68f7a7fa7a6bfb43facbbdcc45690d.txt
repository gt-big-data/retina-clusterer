    "text": "(CNN) -- Concept bars have popped up like daisies in Hong Kong, where big spenders are plentiful but competition is ferocious.\n\nThus, fads (and rental budgets) quickly burn out in this ever-changing city.\n\nIn other words, that amazing bar you hit up on your last business trip might no longer exist.\n\nLooking for a post-meeting pint or a wild night out before you hop on a plane to head home?\n\nHere's a list of the hottest new bars in Hong Kong -- ambiance and creative drinks included.\n\nFor those feeling nostalgic about a time when guys with greased back hair and gals in swing skirts rocked to Elvis blasting on a jukebox, there's Rockabilly, a '50s American bar that stands out from the chaos of Lan Kwai Fong.\n\nFashioned after retro car repair shops and tattoo parlors, the bar meshes American cool with rockabilly chic.\n\nThe concrete and exposed brick walls are covered in bright neon signs, vintage fixtures and hand-drawn charcoal tattoo designs.\n\nRockabilly carries a selection of craft beers from South Africa to Hawaii.\n\nThe Pink Cadillac is tequila based while the Blue Moon is made with white rum.\n\nRockabilly's excellent range of craft beer, Rock 'n' Roll classics play list and simple but great menu has built a loyal following that flocks to the two-story venue.\n\n\"At Rockabilly we stand firm with what we are doing, it's about the way of life and the attitude,\" says manager George Po.\n\nThe bar gets crowded on $10 Tuesdays, Rock-A-Betty Wednesdays and Draft Thursdays.\n\nSignature drink: The craft beer tasting bucket for HK$350 (US$45) comes with six bottles.\n\nLocated in Soho, Little L.A.B.'s hook is that it uses Chinese ingredients in its cocktails.\n\n\"Instead of being trend followers, we want to introduce Chinese elements to the classic recipes,\" says project manager Alex Ng.\n\nThe laboratory concept encourages bartenders to be innovative in evoking authentic Hong Kong flavors in every drink.\n\nKeeping with the bar's lab vibe, Little L.A.B.'s interiors are reminiscent of a sleek and refined home kitchen.\n\nSignature drinks: The Newborn is made with Bacardi Gold & Oakheart rum, homemade Chinese ginger stew, fresh lime juice, fever tree ginger beet and tiki bitters, dressed with a quail egg and sliced ginger.\n\nLocated right across from the Sheung Wan MTR station, The Black Star follows the tradition of London bars located near busy tube stations.\n\nWhen founder Dan Findlay opened both The Black Star and The Blck Brd, he wanted to focus on long-term service and consistent quality to avoid falling victim to Hong Kong's short-lived fads.\n\nThe Black Star differs from its sister bar, being a working neighborhood watering hole that serves gastro-pub style food (notably a 6-ounce prime beef burger and pulled pork sliders) and a streamlined drinks list.\n\nThe simple metal and brick venue is small but maintains a relaxed atmosphere where drinkers can enjoy conversation.\n\n\"It's everything I like about pubs minus everything I dislike about pubs,\" says Findlay.\n\nSignature Drink: Oak barrel aged cocktails, specifically the Old Smokey, essentially an old fashioned with a big chunk of ice and a lemon twist.\n\nIt's rapidly gaining popularity as a hangout in the friendly neighborhood of Tai Hang.\n\nGuay! was inspired by founders (and old friends) Stephanie and Candy's love for drinking and chilling.\n\nThe duo has hands-on involvement in everything from interiors to food (including Stephanie's mom's homemade Peruvian cookies) to the beverage list.\n\nInspired by Stephanie's ancestors' long journey to Peru by wooden ship in the 20th century, the interior and exterior are clad with wood while tile and concrete walls give the space a contrasting industrial feel.\n\nThe drink list includes creative cocktails and international craft beers; there's a small snack menu offering tasty bar bites.\n\nSignature Drink: The fittingly named Graveyard may go down easy, but it packs a punch. It's a mix of an Island base, Everclear, vodka, gin, rum, tequila and a splash of craft beer.\n\nWalking through the heavy doors of Stockton is like stepping into late Victorian era London, complete with a dimly lit alley-like entryway and a narrow wooden staircase.\n\nFounders Malcolm Wood and Matt Reid's eye for detail and fascination with the period is evident in every inch of space.\n\nThe bar is furnished with antique chairs/tables and vintage baccarat crystals, all obtained from Parisian flea markets.\n\nStockton offers award-winning cocktails, boutique champagne and more than 150 whiskies.\n\nThe drink menu is loaded with anecdotes surrounding the origins of each cocktail and a list of \"house rules.\"\n\nFood is on the high end -- lobster, caviar, salmon gravlax and Wagyu beef cheek.\n\nSignature Drink: Brass Monkey is a sweeter twist on the whiskey sour with HM King, compressed citrus, maca, lucuma, vanilla gum and chunco bitters.\n\nThe aptly named, open-air Above is stunning and cool.\n\nPerched atop the new Ovolo Southside Hotel, Above has sweeping views of the playing fields of Wong Chuk Hang and Ocean Park's cable cars that run along the slopes beyond Shouson Hill.\n\nHong Kong's first full warehouse-to-hotel conversion, Ovolo Southside preserves the industrial feel of the original building.\n\nThis keeps with the historic identity of surrounding Wong Chuk Hang, one of the island's last manufacturing districts.\n\nOn the bar's walls, Los Angeles-based graffiti duo Cyrcle has created stunning murals, including a gorgeous piece depicting an imaginary Chinese uprising.\n\nSignature drink: The Next Door's B*tch is a deceivingly sweet cocktail made of vodka, peach liquor, strawberry, basil, rose syrup and lemon juice.\n\nYenn Wong and Jason Atherton, the dynamic duo behind 22 Ships, have teamed up once again to create Ham & Sherry.\n\nBringing Spanish flair to Wan Chai, it's a traditional bodega with an urban twist.\n\nSpanish tiles dress both the bar's interior, while hams dangle overhead.\n\nResembling a traditional tapas bar, Ham & Sherry offers a spacious experience for conversation and movement, with table and bar seating and a lounge area.\n\nThe owners tracked down the world's leading sherry producers to put together Asia's largest sherry list with more than 50 varieties and styles available.\n\nFor food, Michelin-starred chef Jason Atherton provides a selection of aged Spanish ham like Jam\u00f3n Iberico de Bellota and snacks such as Padron peppers.\n\nSignature drink: No prizes for guessing ... it's a sherry! Palo Cortado, to be precise.\n\nInspired by the ship sails and tea leaves of the Hong Kong tea trade, Hong Kong's colonial roots echo in The Envoy.\n\nWhile many of the cocktails are British influenced, creative and fun drinks are also served.\n\nThe award-winning True Blood comes in a faux blood bag inspired by vampires.\n\nThe menu features fusion dishes like Peking Duck rice rolls and deep-fried crab cakes with spicy tomato sauce.\n\nHong Kong's East meets West elements are reflected throughout the bar; the dining area is covered in European-style wallpaper with a Chinese print.\n\nThe Envoy's spaces create an environment in which conversation is valued over loud and flashy entertainment.\n\nSignature drink: Birds of a Feather celebrates the tenacity of migratory birds and is made of Talisker 10 Years, Ballantine's Finest scotch, blueberry and pomegranate tea syrup, blueberry puree, malt essence and lemon juice.\n\nPing Pong 129 Gintoner\u00eda introduces Hong Kong's Sai Ying Pun neighborhood to the Spanish \"gintoner\u00eda,\" a bar that specializes in gin and tonics.\n\nYet Ping Pong isn't overpowered by Spanish influence -- it's more a bridge of Spanish and Hong Kong culture.\n\nLocated in a former ping pong hall, the bar preserves the history and identity of the space by leaving the walls, windows, old-style handrails and original Chinese ping pong equipment ads untouched.\n\nA glowing neon red sign reading \"Keep your body fit\" in Chinese characters hangs above the bar where drinkers can try gin and tonics made from more than 60 brands of gin.\n\nPing Pong carries a selection of craft premium and rare gins produced in distilleries and small producers from all over Spain.\n\nA small section of snacks is available.\n\nSignature drink: The Gin Mare is a Barcelona gin combined with Fever-Tree Tonic, rosemary and lime peel.\n\nMORE: The dirty-fun guide to Hong Kong's Wanchai bars\n\nWith Le Dome de Cristal, Ambrosia Cuisines Group and Louis Roederer's Cristal champagne collaborated to bring Hong Kong the world's first Cristal-branded restaurant.\n\nDesigner Steve Leung channeled Cristal's elegance in creating the bar and restaurant's interiors.\n\nThe circular main dining area has a dark oak wood floor and large windows framing panoramic views of the city.\n\nSoft light illuminates the off-white domed ceiling, warm gray walls and beige drapes, providing a sense of glamor.\n\nDrinkers can order from a selection of 21 Cristal champagnes on the outdoor balcony.\n\nOnce guests exit the creaky old elevator they're greeted by three ceramic statues of Chinese gods Fu (good fortune), Lu (prosperity) and Shou (longevity).\n\nFu Lu Shou is all about giving guests a chance to \"Eat, drink and be prosperous.\"\n\nFrom the mahjong tile bar to the cocktails, it's a clever wink to Hong Kong and Western-Chinese culture.\n\nThe space is funky, retro and usually packed.\n\nDominated by the bar and kitchen, the indoor area is small, so Fu Lu Shou makes full use of its large outdoor terrace, which has comfy plush sofas and swing seats.\n\nThe food emulates Chinatown dishes served on kitschy plates and bowls and includes Western-Chinese classic comfort foods like sweet and sour pork and kung pao chicken.\n\nThe main draw is the drinks, most of them fun and tasty tributes to Hong Kong, with names like Joh Sun, Fuk Yu and Typhoon 8.\n\nYou'll need to call in advance to get the door code, which changes every Tuesday.\n\nSignature drink: Joh Sun is made with lemon-flavored vodka, homemade lemongrass syrup, lemon juice, fresh ginger juice, vinegar, lime and chili.\n\nIt's easy to walk right by Nocturne, the latest whiskey bar to open on Peel Street, without giving it a second look.\n\nThe plain wooden door and concrete facade hide a beautifully minimalist Japanese-style bar with a comprehensive selection of wine and whiskey that's hard to find in Hong Kong.\n\nThe owner, who studied abroad in Japan and has a finance background, travels back once a month to bring back some of the rarest of Japanese whiskies sold only at the distilleries.\n\nHe's opening another Nocturne in Kyoto next summer and two kushiyaki sake bars and a French bar in Hong Kong later this year.\n\nThe selection of Bordeaux vintages and Scotch is superb, but the standouts are the Japanese whiskies, from a single cask Yamazaki or a rare Ichiro's Malt.\n\nSignature drink: An old fashioned made with Japanese whiskey.\n\nWith an intimate environment, the former waiting area of the award-winning Four Seasons' Caprice French restaurant has been converted into a wine and cheese bar.\n\nThough it isn't as recent an opening as others on this list, the place gets a nod because the bar has remained relatively and refreshingly incognito.\n\nLow lighting and comfy chairs make this wholly appropriate for both cozy date nights and cozy client meets -- it's a place for schmoozing and massaging a deal.\n\nThe experience is tailored with exceptional service and, more importantly, a well manicured array of wine and cheese.\n\nThe only criticism is an at times abused climate control -- should the goosebumps arrive for those more scantily clad, staff will happily fetch a selection of shawls.\n\nSignature drink: No real signatures here, but the Champagne Fleury \"Pinot Blanc\" Brut Nature goes very well
