a lid on the seething tensions spilling over from a Belgian bust-up as the German team chases its first world title since 1955.\n\nBut is it too late to mend the poisoned relationship between the star drivers?\n\nWhere did it go wrong?\n\nIn the dry desert heat of Bahrain, rivalries between the Mercedes teammates began to sizzle.\n\nRosberg complained over the team radio when Hamilton cut across him to retake the lead, saying the move was \"not on.\"\n\nMercedes then warned the drivers \"to bring both cars home\" as they vied for victory until the checkered flag.\n\nMonaco was not exactly home sweet home for Rosberg, who lives in the principality.\n\nAfter setting the fastest time in qualifying, the German went off track -- denying Hamilton, who was behind him, a chance to set his potential quickest time.\n\nThere was more drama in Hungary when Mercedes asked Hamilton to allow Rosberg, who was on a different tire strategy, to pass.\n\nThe 2008 world champion, who was second at the time, refused to obey the team order and finished third, one place ahead of Rosberg.\n\nThe pair's duel was short but by no means sweet in Belgium two weeks ago.\n\nIn an ill-judged attempt to take the lead on lap two, Rosberg gave Hamilton's car a punctured tire and effectively put him out of the race.\n\nThe German finished second but was greeted by a chorus of boos on the podium from fans who saw his pass as a deliberately cynical move.\n\nRosberg increasingly finds himself cast as the bad guy, akin to Dick Dastardly in cartoon caper \"Wacky Races.\"\n\nAfter admitting the Spa clash was his fault, Mercedes handed him a hefty fine -- although not by F1 driver salary standards.\n\nBut the 29-year-old, who is always polite and affable in person, has since apologized to Hamilton and told the media melee in Monza this week he was not proud of his behavior.\n\nRosberg may not be Machiavellian but one thing is for sure, Hamilton has had his share of bad luck in 2014.\n\nWhether it was brake failures or engine fires, fate has been a fickle friend.\n\nBut is Hamilton entirely blameless? Well, the Briton has arguably brought some hard luck on himself.\n\nThere have, in particular, been misjudgments in qualifying, most notably in Canada, Austria and Britain, when he chose not to set a final lap when the damp track conditions were improving.\n\nInteractive: Take a tour of the Monza circuit\n\nMercedes wants to calm tensions between its racers as it attempts to win both the driver and constructor titles.\n\nFive days after the Belgium bust-up, the team issued a statement warning \"another such incident will not be tolerated.\"\n\nIt added: \"Lewis and Nico understand and accept the team's number one rule: there must be no contact between the team's cars on track.\"\n\nMercedes motorsport director Toto Wolff said in Italy he was not ruling out changing a driver lineup if \"we are not able to manage the two of them.\"\n\nRosberg recently signed a new contract, which will keep him at the team until the end of 2016, while Hamilton has a deal until 2015.\n\nF1's governing body, the FIA, decided not to investigate the Spa clash, even after Rosberg admitted his culpability.\n\nThere has been debate among F1 experts about whether the FIA should be more proactive in refereeing the heavyweight title bout or whether it should stand by and let the drivers slug it out.\n\nAsked by CNN in Italy whether the FIA should have investigated the Spa incident more closely, Hamilton responded: \"That's a very good question because how do we move forward?\n\n\"Does that mean we can race a lot closer and if the guy in front comes off and is out of the race, nothing's going to happen? Or does it mean if it happens again there will be a penalty?\n\n\"We're always asking to be able to race ... but there's a fine line.\"\n\nWho's in pole position for the title?\n\nWith seven races left to go, Rosberg has a 29-point lead over Hamilton.\n\nHe is on course to win his first world title and follow in the footsteps of his father Keke, the 1982 champion.\n\nHamilton knows this is his best chance of winning a second world title since his triumph with McLaren in 2008.\n\nThe British racer has to keep out of trouble on track and hope his luck turns.\n\nThere is, however, a new twist to the 2014 season, which could be decisive.\n\nThe final race in Abu Dhabi will reward the drivers with double points, which means a win is worth 50 points and second place 36 points.\n\nBut if Rosberg takes a 15-point advantage to the desert finale he can afford to finish second to Hamilton and still win the title.\n\nWhile the two Mercedes drivers are locked in an intense fight, Daniel Ricciardo is sneaking up on the rails.\n\nThe Red Bull racer has the momentum after winning the last two races in Hungary and Belgium and is now 64 points behind Rosberg in the standings.\n\nCould the Australian snatch the title?\n\n\"Yeah I can,\" Ricciardo said in Italy. \"But it obviously is going to take more than just winning the next seven races. It is going to have to take a bit of luck as well.\n\n\"If they crash into each other at every race this year that will probably hand me the championship on a platter!\"\n\nRead: \"It's great to be beating Sebastian Vettel\"\n\nWho can handle the heat?\n\nThere is a feeling in the F1 paddock that the intense 2014 championship is turning into a psychological battle between the Mercedes men.\n\n\"Your mental state is always important in sport,\" Rosberg chirpily agreed in Italy.\n\nThe Mercedes rivals are very different characters and very different racers.\n\nOn one side of the garage is the ice-cool calm and consistency of Rosberg and on the other side is Hamilton's fiery passion and blistering pace.\n\nKeeping a cool head, as well as a steady hand on the wheel, could decide which of the contenders wins the 2014 world title.",
