    "text": "India's Mars Orbiter Mission has entered Mars' orbit, according to the Indian Space Research Organization. India becomes the first nation to arrive in Mars' orbit on its first attempt and the first Asian country to reach the Red Planet.\n\nJust days after NASA's Mars orbiter reached the Red Planet, India's first mission could follow suit and make history Wednesday.\n\nIndia's Mars Orbiter Mission has been groundbreaking for the country, with a price tag of $74 million, a fraction of the $671 million NASA spent on its MAVEN spacecraft.\n\nIf Mars Orbiter Mission, also known as Mangalyaan, successfully enters Mars' orbit, India would become the first Asian nation to do so.\n\nTo date, only the U.S., Europe and the Soviets have successfully sent spacecraft to Mars. But India is also aiming for another record.\n\nIt's aiming to be the first country in the world to succeed in its first attempt to enter the Mars orbit, said S. Satish, a space expert based in Bangalore, India.\n\n\"It's a great technical achievement for the country,\" he said.\n\nOnce it nears Mars' orbit, the spacecraft will have to execute a series of complicated and critical maneuvers. About half of all spacecraft sent to missions on the planet have veered off course, malfunctioned or crashed.\n\n\"The trajectory it has to follow is very complicated,\" Satish said.\n\nThe Mars Orbiter Mission will fire a small rocket in the reverse direction, to reduce speed for exactly 24 minutes and 11 seconds capture into Mars' orbit.\n\nA live telecast will be available starting 6:45 a.m. Indian Standard Time (9:15 p.m. ET) on the Indian Space Research Organization's (ISRO) website. Scientists expect to learn of the outcome around 10:30 p.m. ET.\n\nThe spacecraft launched on November 5, and has traveled over 215 million kilometers to get near Mars. Its mission is to orbit the Red Planet, mapping its surface and studying the atmosphere.\n\nThe mission has been freighted with patriotic significance for India since its inception, and is seen as a symbolic coup over its neighbor, China, which is also ramping up its space ambitions.\n\nChina's joint mission with Russia in 2011, which contained the Chinese Mars satellite, Yinhuo-1 stalled and eventually fell back to Earth. Japan's 1998 attempt with the spacecraft Nozomi was also unsuccessful due to fuel problems.\n\nREAD: Is India's Mars mission the latest escalation in Asia's space race?\n\nOnce it enters the orbit, India's Mars Orbiter Mission will be in the company of NASA's two Mars rovers on the ground, a European orbiter, and the MAVEN, which has been there since Sunday.\n\nThe U.S. has expressed interest in cooperating with India as their spacecraft gather data about the planet.\n\nISRO spent significantly less than NASA on its Mars mission. Some space observers noted that India's Mars orbiter cost less than the $100 million budget for the space thriller \"Gravity.\"\n\n\"It shows how optimal is the design, that way we're able to cut cost and we're not compromising quality,\" Satish said.\n\nCNN's Suzanne Presto and Tim Hume contributed to this report.",
