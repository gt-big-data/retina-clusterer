    "text": "Editor's note: Ray Isle (@islewine on Twitter) is Food & Wine's executive wine editor. We trust his every cork pop and decant -- and the man can sniff out a bargain to boot. Take it away, Ray.\n\n(CNN) -- Summer is done, grilling season is over, life is meaningless, and really, why not just eat lettuce from now on? That was how I was feeling recently, confronted with the sudden awareness of shrinking daylight hours and the first hints of cool weather in the air (note: I live in the Northeast).\n\nBut then, optimist that I am, I thought, what the heck, I'll cook my steaks inside. And, of course, open some good wine.\n\nThere are scads of tips for grilling steaks out there, but for advice for pan-searing and so on, I got in touch with Thomas Dritsas, executive chef of the Del Frisco's group. Since Del Frisco's New York restaurant alone serves more than 1,000 steaks per day (and it's the baby of the bunch\u2014other branches serve 3,000 or more), you have to figure the guy knows something about cooking beef.\n\nHis tips are as follows:\n\n1. Let the steak get to room temperature before you cook it. This keeps the meat from \"seizing\"\u2014the proteins tightening up, leading to a tough steak.\n\n2. Preheat your pan or griddle to 400 degrees. If you don't have a laser thermometer (evidently something executive chefs have; I, like any regular human, do not own one), test the pan by placing a piece of fat on it. It should sizzle and start caramelizing.\n\n3. On a stovetop, don't attempt cuts thicker than one inch. If big ol' Flintstone steaks are your dream, then sear the steak on the stovetop and finish it in a preheated oven.\n\n4. With good beef, salt and pepper are all you really need. And a light coating of olive or canola oil.\n\n5. After cooking, let the steak rest for three to five minutes on a perforated surface, like a cooling rack on a pan or even crumpled-up aluminum foil. You don't want the steak to sit in its juices, because that might lessen the glory of that seared crust you just achieved.\n\nThen, of course, eat the dang thing.\n\nWhich brings us to wine. What is the perfect steak wine, anyway? On a similar tack, I canvassed a number of notable sommeliers for their vision of the perfect (medium-rare, seared) steak wine.\n\nLaura Maniec, a master sommelier who owns the Corkbuzz wine bars, immediately said, \"Old World northern Rh\u00f4ne Syrah. Yum!\"\n\nThat sentiment was echoed by Jordan Salcito, who directs the wine programs for all of chef David Chang's restaurants. \"Cornas in particular,\" she added.\n\nGetting even more specific, Carlton McCoy, head of the wine program at Aspen's Element 47 in the Little Nell Hotel, says, \"Northern Rh\u00f4ne Syrah from a structured vintage with 15 to 20 years of age.\" Which, I have to admit, sounds mighty darn fantastic.\n\nOthers leaned Italian: Jeff Porter, who runs the wine program for NYC's Del Posto restaurant, replied, \"Sangiovese, Sangiovese, Sangiovese\u2014Brunello with a medium-rare steak. Can't beat it!\"\n\nThe Brunello approach was shared by Steven Grubbs, wine director for star chef Hugh Acheson's restaurants: \"Traditional-style Brunello, all day long.\"\n\nKelli White, codirector of the wine program at Napa Valley's Press restaurant, stuck closer to home: \"I like a savory red with some structure and guts to it. Nothing too fruity, and nothing too oaky either\u2014an American Cabernet with some age, let's say.\"\n\nFood & Wine: Best Bacon Burgers in the U.S.\n\nWhat all of these choices share are a substantial tannic structure, reasonably high acidity (perhaps somewhat less so for the Napa wine) and a balance of savory and fruity flavors. This all makes sense: One reason biting into a juicy piece of beef is so pleasurable is the fat content (very few people ask for a dried-out, super-lean steak, and for good reason). Tannins\u2014which can give big red wines an astringent, tongue-drying, raspy quality\u2014do two things in your mouth: they bind with proteins, and their astringent character balances out all that rich fat.\n\nAcidity, similarly, refreshes your palate after a bite of something fatty; that's one reason that super-fruity, low-acid reds actually aren't all that great with a steak, sort of the way Welch's grape juice wouldn't be.\n\nUnfortunately, not everyone can afford (or can raid their restaurant's cellars for) top northern Rh\u00f4ne reds, superstar Brunellos or long-cellared American Cabernets. But, conveniently, all of these regions offer more affordable alternatives.\n\nHere are a few: all, also, out-of-control great with a juicy steak.\n\nMost C\u00f4tes du Rh\u00f4ne is based on Grenache, but Guigal, known for its great C\u00f4te-R\u00f4ties, focuses much more on Syrah. This spicy, inky red definitely hews to the northern Rh\u00f4ne style.\n\nAnother nearly 100 percent Syrah wine from this usually Grenache-based appellation, Saint Cosme's bottling has a lot of meaty depth and distinct herbal notes.\n\nThere's ripe California fruit here, to be sure, but it's balanced by classic Cabernet structure and elegance. And\u2014given how much Martini makes of it\u2014this wine is also surprisingly ageable.\n\nThe baby brother, so to speak, to the more famous Brunello di Montalcino, Rosso di Montalcino comes from exactly the same area, and requires less aging before release. Think vivid cherry fruit, firm structure and juicy acidity.\n\nThis impressive property, owned by the same family that owns the Illy espresso company, produces top-flight Brunellos, and also this ruby-hued, vivid Rosso.\n\nEditor's note: We beg to differ about grilling season being over!
